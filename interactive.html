The Game

Two players jointly control a scalar state $X_t$ over $[0,T]$. Each player privately observes one component of the driving Brownian noise and chooses a control to steer the state toward their own target.

State dynamics

The state evolves as

$$dX_t = \bigl(D_t^1 + D_t^2\bigr)\,dt + dW_t^0, \qquad X_0 = 0,$$

where $W^0$ is common noise observed by neither player.

Private signals

Player $i$ continuously observes a private signal

$$dY_t^i = p_i\,X_t\,dt + dW_t^i, \qquad i=1,2,$$

where $W^1, W^2$ are independent Brownian motions (also independent of $W^0$), and $p_i > 0$ is the signal root-precision (observation precision is $p_i^2$). Player 1 sees only $Y^1$; player 2 sees only $Y^2$. Neither player observes the state $X_t$ directly.

Cost functionals

Player $i$ minimizes

$$J^i = \mathbb{E}\!\int_0^T \!\Bigl[\,(X_t - b_i)^2 + r_i\,(D_t^i)^2\,\Bigr]\,dt,$$

where $b_i$ is player $i$'s bliss point (target state) and $r_i > 0$ is the control cost weight.

Equilibrium concept

We look for a Nash equilibrium amongst all admissible strategies. In equilibrium, each player's control decomposes into a deterministic mean path and a feedback response to their innovation process:

$$D_t^i = \bar{D}^i(t) + \int_0^t D^i(t,s)\,d\widehat{W}^i_t(s),$$

where $\bar{D}^i(t)$ is the mean control, $D^i(t,s)$ is the feedback kernel, and $\widehat{W}^i_t$ is player $i$'s noise state. The solver finds $\bar{D}^1, \bar{D}^2, D^1, D^2$ such that neither player can reduce their cost by deviating.

Mean information wedges V̄¹(t) and V̄²(t). V̄ⁱ(t) prices the marginal value to player i of shifting the opponent's posterior at time t — the additional costate term from bilateral belief manipulation (Theorem 3.2). Both wedges are hump-shaped: zero at t=0 (no inference yet) and t=T (terminal condition on the belief adjoint). Higher opponent precision amplifies the wedge, since a better-informed opponent reacts more sharply to drift perturbations.
Kernel information wedge $\mathcal{V}^1(t,r)$: captures how bilateral belief manipulation distorts the equilibrium impulse response to the $r$-th shock (equation 3.5). Computed as $\mathcal{V}^1(t,r) = p_2^2 \int_0^t \widetilde{X}^2(t,z) \cdot H^{2,1}(t,z,r)\,dz$ where $H^{2,1}$ is the kernel belief-adjoint. The W⁰ channel shows distortion of the fundamental-shock response — the dominant source of excess state variance from strategic friction. Vanishes at $t=T$ by the terminal condition on $H^{2,1}$.
Kernel information wedge $\mathcal{V}^2(t,r)$: symmetric counterpart for player 2, with $1 \leftrightarrow 2$ swapped.

All kernel plots show three sub-panels for noise channels W⁰ (system), W¹ (player 1 observation), W² (player 2 observation). Curves are colored by evaluation time t from purple (early) to yellow (late). Each curve traces the kernel value as a function of source-noise time s ∈ [0, t] — the causal constraint means later-time curves (yellow) extend further.

State kernel X(t,s): the primitive-noise impulse response of the state. The W⁰ channel starts at 1 (direct shock) and decays as both players jointly stabilize older shocks. The W¹ and W² channels are zero at s and build up through drift-based inference as controls feed back through the state.
Player 1's noise-state feedback kernel D¹(t,s): governs how player 1's control at time t responds to their noise-state estimate at source time s (in filtered coordinates). The W⁰ channel dominates because fundamental shocks are most relevant for state tracking; the W¹, W² channels reflect drift-based inference about the opponent's behavior.
Player 2's noise-state feedback kernel D²(t,s): symmetric counterpart to D¹, with players 1↔2 swapped.
Primitive control kernel 𝓓¹(t,s): player 1's control expressed in terms of primitive (unobserved) Brownian shocks W rather than filtered observations. Obtained from D¹ by convolving with the filtration kernel F¹. This is the kernel that enters the state dynamics and determines equilibrium volatility.
Primitive control kernel 𝓓²(t,s): symmetric counterpart to 𝓓¹, with players 1↔2 swapped.
Player 1's unresolved state kernel X̃¹(t,s): the component of the state's impulse response to the s-th shock that player 1 has not yet resolved at time t. Serves as the filtering gain in the noise-state update (Theorem 3.1). Large for recent shocks or weak observation channels, shrinking as observations accumulate. The W¹ channel is identically zero — W¹ enters the state only through player 1's own actions, which propagate deterministically and are measurable to player 1, so they contribute nothing to the unresolved component. (This relies on players having uncorrelated measurements; it need not hold otherwise.)
Player 2's unresolved state kernel X̃²(t,s): symmetric counterpart to X̃¹. The W² channel is identically zero (same reasoning, with players swapped).
Player 1's filtration kernel F¹(T,u,s): the density kernel of the noise-state conditional mean at terminal time (Theorem 3.1). Diagonal blocks (same channel → same channel) tend to dominate; off-diagonal blocks capture cross-channel inference mediated by the state dynamics.
